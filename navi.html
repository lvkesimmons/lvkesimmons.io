NAVI

wearable device for astronauts on board the ISS

10 weeks

group project: responsible for electronics, form, concept development, and user testing

PROBLEM

Astronauts onboard the ISS have a hard time finding objects in storage.

Storage modules are organized with a rack system that houses Cargo Transfer Bags (CTB). Inside each CTB there are many smaller bags. Because everything on the ISS is stored in the CTBs there are over 500 identical CTBs. See the problem?

SOLUTION

NAVI is a wrist worn device that points astronauts towards the item that they are looking for.

The keypad is easily accessible and the LEDs are always visible while floating through the ISS.

A familiar color gradient helps astronauts find the item theyâ€™re looking for.

Each component has an essential purpose and fits precisely into the electronic housing.

PROTOTYPE

NAVI is a fully functioning device and is currently being tested by NASA at the Johnson Space Center in Houston, Texas.

To test NAVI we created a mock storage module. We timed 28 people finding items in CTBs with and without NAVI. Performance improved by 34% while wearing NAVI. It is extremely important for astronauts not to lose any time while in orbit because it is physically and mentally harmful.

To elevate the electronic housing off the body we stretched a band across the bottom plate to create tension.

PROCESS

3D printing allowed us to rapidly prototype and create the slimmest electronic housing possible.

We tested ladderlock, velcro, and clasp closures. None gave us the adjustability and comfort that we wanted. Our strap design was a combination of an inner structure 3D printed in Ninjaflex, wrapped with a fine athletic mesh.

My original concept for the adjustment method was a cabling system, but through user testing we found it created uneven tension.
